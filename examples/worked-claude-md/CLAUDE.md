# CLAUDE.md

## Beat

I cover local government and public infrastructure for the Greenfield
Daily, a five-day-a-week paper serving a city of about 38,000 in the
mid-Atlantic. My main beats are City Council, the city manager's
office, capital projects, and anything that touches the Capital
Improvement Program. I write three to five stories a week plus a
Monday-morning council preview newsletter.

## Key people and institutions

- **Mayor Diana Torres** — first-term mayor, elected 2023. Background in nonprofit housing. Known for press-conference-friendly soundbites; less reliable on technical detail. Good on the record, harder to reach off the record.
- **Council President Walsh** — runs the council meetings. Procedural, careful, rarely tips a vote ahead of time. The person to call when you need to know if an item will actually come to a vote.
- **Council Member Okafor** — fiscal hawk, asks the budget questions everyone else skips. First call for "how does this look on the spreadsheet" stories.
- **Council Member Rivera** — represents the Riverside neighborhood, takes constituent calls seriously, will tell you what residents are saying if you ask plainly.
- **Council Member Kim** — process-focused, asks about contractor selection and bid history. Useful for procurement stories.
- **Council Member Petrov** — institutional memory. Cites past projects unprompted (the 2019 library renovation is his go-to comparison). Skeptical of staff cost estimates.
- **Council Member Goldstein** — youth programming and parks. Friendly but talks in talking points.
- **Council Member Nash** — newest member, focused on grants and outside funding. Worth a call when a state grant is in play.
- **City Manager Harold Briggs** — runs day-to-day operations. Cautious on the record, more useful in person. Approves change orders.
- **Parks Director Lisa Nakamura** — runs the Parks and Recreation Department. Detail-oriented, will walk you through a budget request line by line if you ask.
- **City Engineer Robert Huang** — technical authority on infrastructure projects. Quotable on whether something is "patchable" versus "replace."
- **Alex Martinez** — former council member (2017–2024). Off the council now but still tracks projects, especially anything involving the library renovation he sat through. Useful for historical context. Has not declared whether he's running again.
- **Meridian Infrastructure LLC** — local-ish contractor, did the 2022 Memorial Field renovation. Currently building the $2.3 million Riverside Park drainage and infrastructure project. Lowest qualified bidder.
- **Apex Construction** — second-place bidder on the Riverside Park job at $2.7 million. Worth checking when comparing bids.

## Style

- AP style for all writing
- No Oxford comma (house rule)
- Spell out numbers under 10, figures for 10 and above
- Dollar amounts: $2.3 million, not $2,300,000 or $2.3M
- Use "more than," not "over," for quantities
- Sentence-case headlines and subheads
- "Council member," not "councilman" or "councilwoman"
- "City Council," capitalized when referring to the body; "the council" lowercase on second reference
- First reference uses full name and title; second reference uses last name only
- Times: 7:02 p.m., not 7:02 PM
- "Voted 5-2," not "voted 5 to 2"

## Source standards

- Every claim attributed to a named source unless I have a documented reason on file
- Anonymous sources require sign-off from the city desk editor; I note in the draft why I'm using one
- For dollar amounts, vote counts, and dates, I cite the primary document (meeting minutes, bid packet, budget) and verify against the city's published records
- For contractor history, I check the city procurement database and pull the original RFP before quoting a comparison
- When a source compares a current project to a past one (Petrov's library reference is the typical example), I check the past project's public record before repeating the comparison in print
- Verbatim quotes only when I have audio or my own notes; paraphrase otherwise

## Terminology

- **Capital Improvement Program (CIP)** — the city's multi-year list of funded infrastructure projects, approved annually with the budget. Don't shorten to "the capital plan."
- **Appropriations cycle** — the FY budget approval process, runs January through May in Greenfield.
- **Change order** — a formal contract amendment after a project is underway. Greenfield does not currently require council approval for change orders under $50,000; this was proposed in 2020 but never voted on.
- **Lowest qualified bidder** — the contractor selected through Greenfield's competitive bidding process. "Lowest" alone is misleading; the qualifier matters.
- **Off-cycle vote** — a council vote outside the regular Tuesday meeting schedule, usually for time-sensitive items.
- **Phase one / phase two** — when used by Nakamura or Briggs, refers to construction phasing on a single project. Don't conflate with "fiscal year one / year two."

## Avoid

- Don't use "slammed," "blasted," "ripped," or "torched" for council disagreements. They argue, push back, question, raise concerns. The drama is in the substance, not the verb.
- Don't refer to a contractor as "winning" a contract. They were "selected," "awarded the contract," or "chosen as the lowest qualified bidder."
- Don't say "taxpayers" when "residents" is accurate. Not everyone in Greenfield pays property tax directly.
- Don't repeat the press release framing without checking it against the council minutes. The mayor's office leans optimistic; the minutes are usually more honest about disagreement.
- Don't call a project "controversial" unless I can name at least three people on the record who oppose it.
- Don't describe Petrov as "the lone skeptic" or "the lone dissenter." He's not; he's just the most vocal one.
- Don't use "officials said" without naming the official, even in a paraphrase.
